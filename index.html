Deconstructing a Public Works Project

Welcome, future engineers and project managers. Before the first shovel touches the ground, a public works project is guided by a comprehensive agreement that locks in cost, schedule, and responsibilities. Let’s unpack the Hill Avenue Pathway agreement between the City of Fort Myers and Chris-Tel Construction to see how a multimillion-dollar idea becomes a real pathway.

2. The Scope of Work: What Are We Actually Building?

The Scope of Work narrates the contractor’s obligations. Organized using the CSI MasterFormat divisions, it defines each discipline that turns Hill Avenue into a complete pedestrian and bicycle corridor.

Division 02 — Demolition

Clear the site, remove trees and fencing, and prepare access points exactly as the construction drawings specify.

Division 02 — Surveying

Perform pre-construction surveys, employ ground-penetrating radar to locate underground utilities, and deliver certified as-built documentation when work concludes.

Division 31 — Earthwork

Execute clearing and grubbing, excavation, and embankment operations to establish the correct grades and drainage patterns for the pathway.

Division 32 — Exterior Improvements

Install new wood fencing, signage, asphalt and concrete pavement, pavement markings, extensive landscaping, and the irrigation system—representing the most cost-intensive elements of the project.

Division 33 — Utilities

Adjust stormwater structures and set valve and meter boxes to their final elevations so the new pathway integrates with existing public services.

3. The Rulebook: Key Assumptions and Exclusions

Qualifications and assumptions allocate risk and clarify expectations before the contract is signed. Each clause protects both parties from surprises and keeps the project moving when conditions change.

1. Price Volatility — 30-Day Validity

The GMP is valid for 30 days. Delays in approval shift the risk of market fluctuations to the owner, incentivizing swift execution while shielding the contractor from escalating material and labor costs.

2. Permit Responsibility — Owner’s Duty

City permitting fees are excluded from the contractor’s price. The owner owns the administrative risk, while the contractor focuses on construction risk without absorbing delays in the city’s internal processes.

3. Insurance & Bonds — Included in GMP

Builder’s risk insurance and payment/performance bonds are included, protecting public funds, guaranteeing subcontractor payment, and securing completion if the contractor defaults.

4. Key Exclusions — Managing Unknowns

Environmental testing, design services, and disposal of unsuitable soils are excluded. Unforeseen conditions, such as unexpected geology, are handled through formal change orders instead of hidden contingencies.

4. The Paper Trail: Managing the Project with Essential Forms

Exhibit C of the agreement supplies the templates that guide the project from notice to closeout. Mastering these forms allows project managers to track progress, payments, and accountability with precision.

Notice of Award

Issues the official green light to Chris-Tel Construction, triggering the deadlines for bonds, insurance, and contract execution.

Payment Application

Standardizes the contractor’s monthly request for compensation tied to completed work and stored materials.

Construction Contract Change Order

The formal vehicle for modifying scope, cost, or schedule once construction is underway—no change is official without it.

Certificate of Substantial Completion

Marks the moment the pathway is safe for public use even if minor punch-list items remain open.

Release of Liens

Confirms that subcontractors and suppliers have been paid in full, protecting the City from future claims.

5. Conclusion: Key Lessons from the Hill Avenue Pathway Project

A successful civil project is built first on paper and then on the ground. Internalize these lessons as you prepare to lead multidisciplinary teams and navigate the administrative marathon of public works delivery.

  1. Demand Unambiguous Clarity: Explicitly define cost, scope, and assumptions to prevent disputes and keep owner and contractor aligned.
  2. Master the Administrative Process: Treat documentation as the bedrock of transparency and compliance, not bureaucracy.
  3. Proactively Allocate Risk: Use qualifications and assumptions to decide who owns volatility, permitting delays, and hidden site conditions before construction begins.
